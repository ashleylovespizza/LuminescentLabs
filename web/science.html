Bioluminescence vs Biofluorescence

Bioluminescence is living light produced in an array of glowing organisms. It is a highly efficient chemical reaction that releases mainly light and almost no heat –a.k.a. “cold light.” The phenomenon of bioluminescence is smattered across much of the tree of life.

As opposed to bioluminescence, biofluorescence is not a chemical reaction, instead it involves special pigments that absorb light of a certain color and then re-emit it at a different color. In the process, some energy absorbed from the light is lost as heat. So the outgoing light has less energy- therefore longer wavelength- than the light coming in. What this means is that a fluorescent molecule is “excited” with light with a color that is more towards the blue end of the light spectra (i.e. rainbow) than the light that it gives off. The change in wavelength translates to a change in color.

The Science of Bioluminescence

Bioluminescence is produced by the intermingling and interaction of three chemicals: luciferin, luciferase, and oxygen.

Some organisms generate their own light through a “photoprotein,” which emits light in an oxygen-independent reaction, as it already has the oxygen built in.

The Science of Biofluorescence

Fluorescent light is only seen while the specimen is being illuminated.

On an atomic level, fluorescent molecules do not produce their own light; they absorb photons, which temporarily excite electrons to a higher energy state. As these electrons quickly relax to their ground state, they release their energy, usually at a longer wavelength. Excitation and relaxation occurs almost instantaneously (within a trillionth to a millionth of a second).
